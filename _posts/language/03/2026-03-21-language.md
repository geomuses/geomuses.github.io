Happiness Report Finds Worrying Trends

A society that supports freedom, equality and justice. That's the secret to a happy country, according to Finland's President Alexander Stubb.

The country has just been named the world's happiest country for the ninth year in a row.

Other Nordic countries — including Denmark, Iceland, Sweden and Norway — also ranked highly in this year's World Happiness Report.

Researchers say these countries benefit from strong welfare systems, good health care and high levels of trust in society.

Costa Rica was a new entry in the top five, rising to fourth place. Experts say this may be due to strong family relationships and social connections that people living there enjoy.

The report is based on surveys of around 100,000 people in 140 countries. People were asked to rate their lives on a scale from 0 to 10.

However, the report also highlighted a worrying trend among young people, especially in English-speaking countries and Western Europe.

Life satisfaction among people under 25 has fallen over the last 10 years. Researchers say that heavy use of social media may be one reason for this.

The effect appears to be strongest among teenage girls. The report found that 15-year-old girls who use social media for five hours or more each day report lower levels of happiness.

At the same time, young people who use social media for less than one hour a day report the highest levels of well-being — even higher than those who do not use it at all.

Researchers say that some platforms may have a more negative effect because they focus on pictures, influencers and algorithm-based feeds. These can lead to unhealthy comparisons with others.

But the link between social media and well-being is not the same everywhere. In some regions, such as the Middle East and South America, youth well-being has not fallen despite heavy social media use.
