# US Adults Say Being Informed Matters, Few Say They Are

The majority of US adults believe it's "extremely" or "very" important for people to stay informed about a range of topics that affect the country.

For example, 82% say it's either extremely or very important to be informed about the country's healthcare system, with the same percentage saying the same about being aware of voting and election processes.

However, fewer Americans say they actually are extremely or very informed about these topics themselves, according to a March 2026 survey by Pew Research Center.

So even though a large majority believe it's important to be informed about the healthcare system, only 34% feel they are. A higher percentage — 50% — believe they're extremely or very well-informed about voting and election processes.

The Pew survey asked around 1,000 US adults about 11 key topics.

The pattern continues across all 11 topics, with 80% thinking it's extremely or very important to know what their president can or cannot do, but only 36% feeling they're that well informed themselves.

When it comes to economic and tax policies, 75% think a high level of knowledge is important, but only 24% feel highly informed.

Pew also looked at where US adults are getting their information on these topics.

Traditional news media is the most popular main source for all of the topics, although it's tied with government sources for information about social security. But 51% of participants name traditional news media as their main source of information on US foreign policy.

Social media is also a popular source of information for Americans on some topics, with 19% saying they use it to learn about government regulation of technology and media companies. Only 10% say they use government sources to learn about this topic.

A relatively small number of people say formal education is a main source of their information. However, 18% say it's how they know the limits of what a US president can do.
