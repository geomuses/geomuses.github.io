# Middle East Conflict Threatens Japan Banana Crisis

The recent conflict in the Middle East has caused a variety of problems around the world, including higher energy prices and shortages of supplies. In Japan, which depends heavily on Middle Eastern oil, the government is even creating an extra budget to manage the impact of such problems.

But now the situation in the Middle East may be causing a new supply crisis for Japan — a shortage of bananas.

If you're wondering how war in the Middle East could affect the supply of bananas, the answer is a little complicated.

Japan imports its bananas while they're still green, and then uses a gas called ethylene to ripen the fruit so they're a nice, fresh yellow when they arrive at local supermarkets. These bananas can't ripen properly without ethylene.

But that gas is derived from a liquid called naphtha, which itself is derived from crude oil, and is normally used to make products like paint and plastic.

According to _Bloomberg_, Japan's naphtha supplies are down by a quarter so far this year because of the closing of the Strait of Hormuz. And this is affecting various industries — not just bananas.

For example, the snack company Calbee is now using black and white packets for its potato chips because of a shortage of colored ink, which is derived from naphtha.

So without naphtha, there isn't enough ethylene, and without ethylene, cut bananas will just stay hard, green and unsellable until they rot.

Bananas haven't disappeared from Japanese supermarkets yet, and Japan's fruit-ripening companies are trying to find new ways to get ethylene.

Meanwhile, Eiji Akashi of the Japan Banana Importers Association told _Bloomberg_ that there may be enough ethylene to last for two or three more months.

"Prices may rise, but we're doing everything we can to avoid shortages," Akashi said.
