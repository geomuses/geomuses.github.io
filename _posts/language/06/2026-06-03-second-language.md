# Influencer Work on Tourist Visas Banned in Bali

The Indonesian island of Bali welcomes over 7 million foreign visitors every year. People are attracted to it for its beaches, food and local culture.

For many visitors, Bali also offers relatively low-cost luxury accommodation and experiences, all in a beautiful landscape.

Along with regular vacationers, Bali has become a hotspot for influencers, who Indonesian immigration officials have now announced cannot use a regular tourist visa.

A tourist visa bans the holder from working in Bali in any way. The local immigration authorities have made it clear that this includes making sponsored social media posts, doing brand collaborations and taking part in photo shoots.

An influencer doesn't have to be paid directly with money to break the rules either. A hotel stay given in exchange for a social media post is banned too.

In fact, any activity that has economic value — whether money is directly involved or not — can be treated as work under the country's immigration law. This also means a wide range of activities that people might not expect could be banned.

Things like playing a DJ set, teaching a free yoga class or even volunteering could result in immigration authorities getting involved, if it seems like there is some economic value in the activity.

The rules are being backed up by action too. The local authorities are now regularly checking social media, reviewing complaints from residents and taking part in organized raids.

People found breaking the rules of their visa face fines of up to around $28,000, deportation and a ban on returning — and even jail time.

This doesn't mean influencers can't visit Bali for work, though, as last summer the Indonesian government announced a new visa just for social media creators.

The C5A content creator visa offers influencers 60 days — which can be increased twice for up to 180 days in total — for all the paid posts and free stays they could want.
