# Japan Designates Broccoli an Essential Vegetable

The Japanese government has added broccoli to its list of "designated vegetables," which are seen as an essential part of the Japanese diet.

Designated vegetables are those that are in high demand and are therefore seen as necessary to daily life. This status means that farmers who produce these vegetables will be given government assistance if prices fall. That way, farmers can keep growing enough to meet demand.

The government began choosing designated vegetables in 1966, but broccoli is the first new item on the list since potatoes were added in 1974.

There are now 15 designated vegetables on the government list, including cabbage, cucumber, _satoimo_ taro, daikon radish, tomato, eggplant, carrot and green onion — and now broccoli!

As the Japanese population grows smaller, production of other vegetables has mostly stayed the same or decreased in recent years. But according to government data, demand for broccoli is increasing.

In 1990, sales of broccoli were 540 grams per person each year. But by 2022 that had risen to 1,619 grams per person.

So why has broccoli become so popular?

One reason is probably its mild taste. It's also very easy to cook, and it lasts a long time in the fridge.

But it could also be that people are more aware of broccoli's health benefits. Broccoli contains lots of vitamins, calcium and fiber, and is also a good source of protein. There is also a compound in broccoli called sulforaphane, which research has linked to better heart health and cancer prevention.
