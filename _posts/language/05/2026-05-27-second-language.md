Many Workers Don't Want to Be Managers — Survey

People often talk about the "career ladder." This refers to the idea of joining a company and slowly moving up. So you might start as an intern, with the hope of becoming a paid employee, and then a manager after some time.

But according to a new survey, many young people don't want to be managers at all.

Research company Ipsos Karian and Box surveyed 5,000 people in the UK who work for large companies.

Only 15% of those surveyed said the idea of becoming a manager is attractive to them, while more than 60% said the opposite.

And 40% of participants said that the day-to-day work of managers in their companies does not look appealing.

Many believe that managers have a lot more responsibility today than they did in the past, and that could be part of the reason why many find the role unappealing.

Fewer people are seeing the role as the next step in their career, and instead are seeing it as a step into more responsibility and difficulty.

In fact, more than a quarter of managers who were surveyed said they have at least 10 things expected of them every day, and a similar percentage said they find a lot of these expectations unmanageable.

Some experts say another problem is that many managers are "accidental" and are not given proper training. A different survey done in 2023 found that 82% of managers in the UK said they had no training before entering the role.

Jack Evans from Ipsos Karian and Box said these results show that there is a big problem, and that employers are risking not having enough managers to keep their companies running in the future.

He added that employers should think more about what they are asking of their managers to make their career ladder more appealing.
