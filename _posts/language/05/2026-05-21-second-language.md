# Japan's Child Population Falls to New Low

Japan's child population has fallen again, and the country now has fewer children than ever before.

According to government data, the estimated population of children under the age of 15 was 13.3 million on April 1. That includes 6.8 million boys and 6.5 million girls.

The total number of children fell by 360,000 from the year before.

This decline is not new. In fact, it was the forty-fifth straight year that Japan's child population has fallen. It is also the lowest number of children since records began in 1950.

Looking at children in three-year age groups, the trend of fewer children being born is clear. Those aged 12 to 14 were the largest group, at 3.1 million, while those aged 0 to 2 were the smallest, at 2.1 million.

Children aged under 15 in Japan now make up only 10.8% of the country's total population — a figure that has also been falling for decades.

Compared with other large countries, Japan has one of the world's lowest percentages of children. Among countries with populations above 40 million, only South Korea has a lower percentage, at 10.2%.

Meanwhile, the percentage of older people in Japan continues to increase. People aged 65 and older now total 36.2 million, representing 29.5% of the population.

According to government estimates, Japan's population is expected to fall by about 30% by 2070, when four out of every 10 people will be 65 or older.
